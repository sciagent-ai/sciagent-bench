### Why the MFE Falls Short of 25% — Root Cause

**Zone 1's T+1 shortfall at large negative angles is the primary driver.** At θ_x = −10°, the Zone 1 T+1 is 72.9% (vs. ~96% paper target). The paper achieves higher T+1 in Zone 1 through two mechanisms not fully captured here:

1. **True 2D circular pillar geometry**: The paper uses an actual circular pillar (optimized by RSoft with full 2D Fourier basis). S4's approximation of the circle as a rectangle reduces mode-matching accuracy at large angles, where the shape anisotropy matters most.

2. **Material dispersion refinement**: The paper uses ALD-grown TiO2 with n ≈ 2.42–2.45 at 532 nm (higher than the n=2.35 used here), which shifts resonances and lifts peak T+1.

3. **Zone 1 design intent**: The paper explicitly states Zone 1 has a merit function that *balances* efficiency at both FOV ends (not just maximize at −10°). The full RSoft MOST optimizer ran many more iterations with a more sensitive merit function than the Nelder-Mead run here.

4. **Simulation model accuracy**: The S4 simulation is fundamentally 1D RCWA (single Fourier direction), while the nano-pillar requires a full 2D simulation. The paper used RSoft with proper 2D RCWA. For Zones 2 and 3 (which have larger pillars and broader angular bandwidths), this approximation is less damaging — which is why their efficiencies match targets well (within 3–4%).

The shape of the efficiency-vs-angle curves (Zone 1: high T+1/low R0, dropping toward +10°; Zone 3: flat, low T+1/high R0) **reproduces Fig. 3(d–f) qualitatively correctly**, confirming the physical model is right. The 4.9 pp gap in MFE is a quantitative accuracy limitation of the 1D S4 geometry approximation for Zone 1's critical performance at −10°.
